Fly Ash Based Geopolymer Concrete: Properties, Mix Design, and Applications

📝 Er. P. Durga Prasad 📅 June 25, 2026 ⏱️ 12 min read

Among various source materials used for synthesizing geopolymer concrete, Fly Ash is the most widely studied and commercially utilized. Fly ash is a fine powder byproduct generated by coal-fired thermal power plants. Because it consists of spherical glass particles rich in active silica (SiO₂) and alumina (Al₂O₃), it is an ideal precursor for the geopolymerization process. Replacing Portland cement with fly ash not only solves a massive landfill disposal issue but also creates a high-performance binder with unique properties.

In this article, we will examine the chemical formulation of fly ash geopolymers, the steps to design a mix, the impact of curing temperatures, and their practical structural applications.

Class F vs. Class C Fly Ash

The chemical composition of fly ash varies depending on the source coal. ASTM C618 classifies fly ash into two primary types:

  • Class F Fly Ash: Derived from anthracite or bituminous coal. It contains low calcium (less than 10% CaO) and high silica/alumina content (greater than 70%). Class F is the preferred precursor for geopolymer concrete because the absence of calcium allows for a stable polymerization process, resulting in superior acid and heat resistance. It generally requires heat curing to activate.
  • Class C Fly Ash: Derived from lignite or sub-bituminous coal. It contains high calcium (greater than 20% CaO). While it sets faster at room temperature due to simultaneous C-S-H hydration, the rapid setting can make workability difficult to control, and it has lower long-term chemical durability compared to Class F.

Formulating the Activator Solution

Unlike concrete mix design with water and cement, geopolymer design requires selecting chemical proportions for the activating solution. The solution typically combines Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) and Sodium Silicate (Na₂SiO₃).

1. Sodium Hydroxide Molarity

The concentration of NaOH is measured in Molarity (M), representing moles of solute per liter of solution. Higher molarity (e.g., 10M to 16M) provides more hydroxyl ions (OH⁻) to dissolve the silica and alumina in fly ash, which increases the early compressive strength of the concrete. However, concentrations above 16M can cause excessive fast-setting and reduce final strengths due to ion congestion.

2. Sodium Silicate to Sodium Hydroxide Ratio

The ratio of Na₂SiO₃ liquid to NaOH liquid by mass typically ranges from 1.5 to 2.5. Na₂SiO₃ acts as a soluble silica source, which accelerates the polycondensation reaction. A ratio of 2.0 to 2.5 is widely considered optimal for compressive strength.

3. Alkaline Liquid to Fly Ash Ratio

The ratio of total activator liquid (NaOH + Na₂SiO₃) to fly ash by mass typically ranges from 0.30 to 0.45. Lower ratios increase compressive strength but severely reduce workability (yielding a stiff, sticky mix).

Curing Temperature and Time

For Class F fly ash geopolymers, curing conditions play a critical role in strength development:

  • Elevated Temperature Curing: Curing concrete at 60°C to 90°C for 24 hours accelerates the chemical reaction. The high temperature supplies the activation energy needed to break the strong Si-O and Al-O bonds in fly ash. Once heated, the concrete can reach up to 70% of its ultimate strength within 24 hours.
  • Ambient Curing: Curing at room temperature (25°C to 30°C) is preferred for cast-in-place concrete. To achieve this, engineers blend a small percentage of GGBS (10% to 30%) with fly ash. The calcium in GGBS generates exothermic heat during hydration, providing the local energy needed to trigger fly ash geopolymerization without external steam.

Industrial & Structural Applications

Because GPC has high early strength, rapid set options, and excellent durability, it is highly suited for several specialized civil engineering applications:

1. Precast Concrete Pipes and Blocks

Precast yards are ideal for fly ash geopolymers because the steam-curing chambers already exist. Geopolymer sewer pipes are highly resistant to sulfuric acid attacks (microbially induced corrosion) that typically destroy standard OPC sewer pipes within a few decades.

2. Marine Infrastructure

GPC's dense polymer matrix prevents chloride ions from penetrating the concrete and corroding internal steel rebar. This makes it perfect for retaining walls, docks, and offshore foundation blocks.

3. High-Temperature Chimneys and Refractories

Because geopolymer binders do not contain calcium hydroxide, they do not decompose at high temperatures. They perform exceptionally well in industrial furnace foundations, chimneys, and fire-resistant panels.

Key Takeaways

  • Class F Fly Ash (low-calcium) is the industry standard precursor for durable geopolymer concrete.
  • The Sodium Silicate to Sodium Hydroxide ratio is typically designed between 1.5 and 2.5.
  • Class F geopolymers generally require heat curing (60°C - 90°C) to achieve high strengths, unless blended with GGBS.
  • GPC's resistance to acids, sulfates, and fire makes it highly superior to OPC for marine and industrial structures.
