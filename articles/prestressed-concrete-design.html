Prestressed Concrete Design: Principles, Methods, and Modern Applications

📝 Er. P. Durga Prasad 📅 July 12, 2026 ⏱️ 13 min read

Reinforced concrete (RCC) is a highly successful structural composite, combining the compressive strength of concrete with the tensile strength of steel rebar. However, for a reinforced concrete beam to engage its steel reinforcement in tension, the surrounding concrete must first crack. Under large structural spans or heavy loads, this cracking becomes severe, leading to high deflections and water-ingress risks. To overcome these limitations, structural engineers use **Prestressed Concrete (PSC)**, actively applying compressive forces to the concrete member before it carries operational service loads.

In this article, we will examine the physical principles of prestressing, compare pre-tensioning versus post-tensioning, outline sources of prestress loss, and look at their practical structural applications.

The Principle of Prestressing

The core concept of prestressing is simple: **neutralize tensile stresses with pre-applied compression**. Consider a simple concrete beam under gravity loads. Bending causes tension in the bottom fibers and compression in the top. If we run a high-tensile steel tendon through the bottom of the beam and stretch (tension) it, the steel pulls back, placing the bottom concrete fibers under pre-compression.

When the gravity loads are applied, the bending tension must first overcome this pre-existing compression before it can place the concrete in tension. By optimizing the tendon profile and force, we can design a beam that experiences strictly compressive stresses across its entire cross-section under full service loads — resulting in a crack-free, highly rigid structural member.

Pre-tensioning vs. Post-tensioning

Prestressing forces are applied using two distinct methodologies:

1. Pre-tensioning

In pre-tensioning, the steel tendons are anchored and stretched between external bulkheads in a casting bed **before** the concrete is poured. Once the concrete is poured and reaches the required strength (typically 3 to 7 days), the tendons are cut from the bulkheads. As the cut steel tries to contract, the force is transferred to the concrete via bond stress along the tendon length. This method is highly suited for factory precast yards producing standardized members like railway sleepers, electrical poles, and double-T floor slabs.

2. Post-tensioning

In post-tensioning, the concrete member is cast with hollow ducts running through it. The steel tendons are threaded through these ducts **after** the concrete has hardened. Once the concrete reaches its design strength, hydraulic jacks anchor to one end of the concrete member and pull the tendons. Once fully tensioned, the tendons are locked in place using mechanical wedges (anchorages). Post-tensioning is conducted directly on-site and is ideal for cast-in-place long-span bridge girders, foundation mats, and flat slabs in commercial buildings.

High-Strength Materials

Prestressed concrete requires materials of much higher strength than standard RCC:

  • Concrete Grade: Minimum grade of concrete is **M30** for post-tensioned members and **M40** for pre-tensioned members (IS 1343:2012). High-strength concrete is essential to resist the massive anchorage zone compressive stresses and to minimize prestress losses caused by concrete creep and shrinkage.
  • Steel Tendons: Standard mild steel bars (Fe 250 or Fe 415) cannot be used because they would lose all prestressing force due to concrete shrinkage. PSC uses high-tensile steel wires and strands with ultimate tensile strengths (UTS) ranging from **1500 to 1860 MPa** (roughly 4 times stronger than standard rebar).

Understanding Prestress Loss

The initial prestress force applied by hydraulic jacks reduces over time. Calculating this reduction is a critical step in PSC design. Prestress losses are divided into two categories:

Immediate Losses

  • Elastic Shortening of Concrete: As the prestressing force is transferred, the concrete compresses and shortens, causing the tendon to relax slightly.
  • Anchorage Slip: When the hydraulic jack releases the tendon, the locking wedges slide a few millimeters into the anchorage before securing the steel, losing a fraction of tension.
  • Friction (Post-tensioning only): Friction between the steel tendon and the surrounding duct walls as the tendon is pulled, particularly if the duct is curved.

Long-Term (Time-Dependent) Losses

  • Concrete Creep: Ongoing plastic deformation of concrete under sustained compressive stress over years.
  • Concrete Shrinkage: Loss of moisture as concrete cures and ages, causing it to shrink in volume.
  • Relaxation of Steel: The slow reduction of tension in steel strands kept under constant strain over time (similar to the loosening of a guitar string).

Total prestress losses typically range from **15% to 25%** of the initial jacking force. Designers must calculate both the *initial prestress* (for transfer stage check) and the *effective prestress* (for service load checks).

Structural Applications

PSC's ability to span long distances while staying thin and crack-free makes it indispensable for:

  • Long-Span Bridges: Segmental box girders and cable-stayed bridge decks.
  • Post-Tensioned Flat Slabs: Allows thin floor slabs in commercial high-rises, maximizing floor-to-ceiling heights and reducing overall structural weight.
  • Containment Vessels: Nuclear reactor shields and massive cylindrical water tanks where crack-free liquid containment is critical.

Key Takeaways

  • Prestressing pre-applies compressive stress to concrete to prevent tension cracks under service loads.
  • Pre-tensioning stretches tendons before pouring concrete (bond transfer); **Post-tensioning** stretches tendons after concrete hardens (anchorage transfer).
  • Requires high-strength materials: minimum concrete grade of M30/M40 and high-tensile steel tendons (1500+ MPa).
  • Designers must account for 15% to 25% losses in prestress force due to concrete creep, shrinkage, and steel relaxation over the structure's lifetime.
